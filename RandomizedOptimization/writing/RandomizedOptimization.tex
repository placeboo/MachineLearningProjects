\section{Introduction}
This report explores the performance of randomized optimization algorithms in two domains: discrete optimization problems and neural network weight optimization. Sec. \ref{sec:optimization-problems-n-queens} and \ref{sec:four-peaks}focus on two discrete optimization problems: N-Queens and Four-Peaks, designed to highlight the advantages of Simulated Annealing(\sa) and Genetic Algorithm (\ga), respectively. I have implemented Random Hill Climbing (\rhc), \sa\, \ga\, and \mimic\ to solve these problems, comparing their performance across different problem sizes and hyperparameters. Sec. \ref{sec:neural-network} applies these algorithms to optimize the weights of a neural network from Assignment 1, using the YouTube comment spam detection dataset, and contrasts them with traditional backpropagation.

\section{Discrete Optimization Problems: N-Queens\label{sec:optimization-problems-n-queens}}

The N-Queens problem is a discrete optimization task that aims to place $N$ queens on an $N \times N$ chessboard without any queens attacking each other \cite{b1}. The fitness function maximizes the number of non-attacking queen pairs. This problem is interesting,
\textbf{Complexity}: The number of possible states grows exponentially with board size $N$, making it NP-hard;
\textbf{Local Optima}: N-Queens has many local optima, increasing with the board size.
Based on the algorithms' characteristics, I \textbf{hypothesize}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \sa\ will outperform \rhc\ and \ga\ in terms of solution quality for larger board sizes due to its ability to escape local optima in the complex N-Queens landscape.
    \item \ga\ will show the fastest initial convergence but may struggle with larger board sizes due to the constraints making crossover operations less effective.
    \item \rhc\ will perform well for smaller board sizes but struggle as the problem size increases due to the growing number of local optima.
    \item \ga's execution time will increase more rapidly with problem size compared to \sa\ and \rhc, due to the complexity of maintaining a valid population of solutions.
    \item For \sa, a Geometric Decay cooling schedule will outperform Arithmetic and Exponential Decay schedules in solution quality, because it allows more exploration in the early stages.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Experimental Setup\label{sec:nqueens-experimental-setup}}
I implemented the experiments using the \texttt{mlrose-ky} library, defining a custom fitness function that calculates the logarithm of non-attacking queen pairs. The experiments were structured as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Size Experiment}: Tested algorithms on board sizes N = 8, 16, 24, ..., 120:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Algorithms: \rhc, 
        \sa, \ga
        \item Fixed Parameters: max iterations = 1000, max attempts = 100, random seed = 42;
        \item Algorithm-specific Parameters: \rhc\ (10 restarts), \sa\ (exponential decay), \ga\ (population size = 200, mutation probability = 0.1)
    \end{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Hyperparameters Experiment}: Fixed problem size N = 56, max iterations = 1000, max attempts = 100 (except for \sa), random seed = 42:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \rhc:  Restarts: $[5, 10, 20]$
        \item \sa: Schedule: [\geomD, \expD, \arithD];  Maximum attempts: $[10, 20, 50, 100]$
        \item \ga\: Population size: $[100, 200, 400]$; Mutation probability: $[0.1, 0.2, 0.3, 0.4]$
    \end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}

Fig. \ref{fig: nqueens-performance-metrics} show the performance metrics of the algorithms (\rhc, \sa , \ga) on the N-Queens problem across different board sizes. The results both support and challenge my initial hypotheses. Contrary to our first hypothesis, \sa\ only outperforms \rhc\ and \ga\ for smaller board sizes ($N \leq 40$). This is likely due to \sa's ability to escape local optima being more effective in smaller search spaces. For larger boards, the vast number of possible configurations may limit \sa's advantage.
\ga\ consistently outperforms both \rhc and \sa\ by 0.1\% in fitness value for larger board sizes, contradicting part of our second hypothesis. This suggests that \ga's population-based approach is more effective at finding global optima in larger search spaces, despite the constraints of the N-Queens problem. However, supporting the second part of this hypothesis, GA exhibits the fastest initial convergence (Fig. \ref{fig: nqueens-fitness-vs-iteration}).
Confirming our third hypothesis, \rhc struggles with larger problem sizes. It starts with the lowest fitness value and gets stuck at iterations 100-200, indicating it's trapped in local optima before finding better solutions through restarts. This behavior is expected due to the increasing number of local optima as the board size grows.
My fourth hypothesis about execution time is strongly supported. \ga\ is indeed the slowest algorithm, with execution time increasing exponentially (Fig.  \ref{fig: nqueens-execution-time-vs-size}). At board size 120, \ga\ takes 227 seconds, while \sa\ takes only 3 seconds, and \rhc\ takes 22 seconds. This is because \ga's crossover and mutation operations become increasingly complex for larger board sizes, as maintaining valid solutions becomes more challenging. \sa\ emerges as the fastest algorithm, maintaining efficiency even at larger board sizes. This wasn't explicitly hypothesized but can be explained by \sa's simple operation of moving queens to new positions, which remains efficient regardless of board size.
Fig. \ref{fig: nqueens-iterations-vs-time-vs-size} reveals that \ga\ converges in the fewest iterations, but each iteration is computationally expensive. \rhc\ and \ga\ use more iterations as board size increases, while \sa\ always stops at 1000 iterations. This suggests that increasing \sa's max iteration count might improve its performance for larger problems.

These results highlight the trade-offs between solution quality and computational efficiency. While \ga\ achieves slightly better fitness values for larger board sizes, its computational cost is significant. \sa's balance of good solutions and fast execution time makes it particularly suitable for the N-Queens problem, especially at moderate scales. The problem's structure, with its many local optima, particularly benefits \sa's ability to escape local optima by accepting worse solutions based on its temperature schedule.

\section{Discrete Optimization Problems: Four-Peaks \label{sec:four-peaks}}
The four-peak problem operates on a string bit $x$, aiming to maximize a fitness function based on consecutive 1s and 0s, and a threshold $T$ \cite{b2}. 
\begin{align}
    f(x, T) = \max(\text{tail}(0, x), \text{head}(1, x)) + R(x, T)\label{eq: 4-peak-fitness}
\end{align}
where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\text{tail}(b, x)$ is the number of trailing $b$'s in $x$;
    \item $\text{head}(b, x)$ is the number of leading $b$'s in $x$;
    \item $R(x, T) = n$, if $\text{tail}(0, x) > T$ and $\text{head}(1, x) > T$; and
    \item $R(x, T) = 0$, otherwise.
\end{itemize}
This problem is interesting due to its fitness landscape, characterized by four "peaks": two local optima and two global optima. For a string length of 8 and threshold $T = 2$:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Local optima: 11111111 and 00000000 (fitness = 8);
    \item Global optima: 11111000 and 00011111 (fitness = 13).
\end{itemize}
Based on the problem characteristics and algorithm properties, I \textbf{hypothesize}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \ga\ will outperforms \rhc\, \sa\ in terms of fitness value, as it's more likely to find global optima in the multi-peak landscape.
    \item \mimic\ will show better performance compared with \ga\, as it maintains a diverse population and is likely to find global optima.
    \item The performance gap between population-based methods (\ga\ and \mimic) and single-solution methods (\rhc\ and \sa) will widen as the problem size increases.
    \item For \ga\, larger population sizes combined with moderate mutation rates will yield better results than smaller populations or extreme mutation rates.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
I implemented the experiments using the \texttt{mlrose\_ky} library with the built-in FourPeaks fitness function. 

\subsubsection{Size Experiment \label{sec:four-peaks-size-experiment}}
I tested the algorithms on a range of problem sizes, from 10 to 150 bits, incrementing by 10, and set the thresholds percentage $t\_pct = 0.1$. The experiments were structured as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Algorithms: \rhc, \sa, \ga, \mimic
    \item Fixed Parameters: maximum iterations = 1000, maximum attempts = 100, random seed = 42, $t\_pct = 0.1$
    \item Algorithm-specific Parameters: \rhc\ (10 restarts), \sa\ (exponential decay), \ga\ (population size = 200, mutation probability = 0.1), \mimic\ (population size = 200, keep percent = 0.2)
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Hyperparameter Experiment \label{sec:four-peaks-hyperparameter-experiment}}
Fixed problem size of 50 bits, max iterations = 1000, and random seed = 42, I tested the algorithms with different hyperparameters:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \rhc: Restarts: [5, 10, 20]
    \item \sa: Schedule: [\expD, \arithD, \geomD]; Maximum attempts: [10, 20, 50, 100]
    \item \ga: Population size: [100, 200, 400]; Mutation probability: [0.1, 0.2, 0.3, 0.4]
    \item \mimic: Population size: [100, 200, 400]; Keep percent: [0.1, 0.2, 0.3, 0.4]
\end{itemize}
For each experiment, 
I collected data on best fitness achieved, execution time, number of iterations, and the fitness curve over iterations.

\subsection{Results}
\textbf{Performance across Problem Sizes}. Figure \ref{fig: four-peaks-performance-metrics} illustrates the performance metrics of \rhc, \sa, \ga, and \mimic on the Four-Peaks problem across different bit lengths. The results both support and challenge our initial hypotheses.
Supporting our first hypothesis, \ga\ consistently achieves higher fitness values than \rhc\ and \sa\ for most problem sizes. This aligns with our expectation that \ga's population-based approach is more effective at navigating the multi-peak landscape of the Four-Peaks problem. \ga's performance shows some fluctuations, particularly at bit lengths 100 and 110 in Fig. \ref{fig: four-peaks-best-fitness-vs-size}, likely due to the increased complexity of the search space. Contrary to our second hypothesis, \mimic\ does not consistently outperform \ga. While \mimic\ demonstrates rapid initial improvement and achieves the highest fitness for some problem sizes ($N=100, 110, 140$), it doesn't consistently maintain this advantage across all sizes. This suggests that while \mimic's approach of maintaining a diverse population is effective, it may not always be superior to \ga's evolutionary approach in finding global optima for this problem. My third hypothesis is strongly supported by the results. The performance gap between population-based methods (\ga\ and \mimic) and single-solution methods (\rhc\ and \sa) indeed widens as the problem size increases. This is evident from the diverging fitness values and the inability of \rhc\ and \sa\ to improve performance for larger problem sizes in Fig. \ref{fig: four-peaks-best-fitness-vs-size}. \rhc\ and \sa's best fitness scores reduce when bit lengths exceed 40 and 60, respectively, suggesting they get stuck in local optima as problem size increases. 
Examining execution times (Fig.  \ref{fig: four-peaks-execution-time-vs-size}) reveals that \mimic\ is significantly slower than the other algorithms, with execution time increasing exponentially as problem size grows. This is due to the computational complexity of building and sampling from its probabilistic model. \ga\ also shows an upward trend in execution time, albeit less pronounced, which is expected given its population-based nature.
The fitness curves for a 50-bit problem (Fig. \ref{fig: four-peaks-best-fitness-vs-size}) provide insights into convergence behavior. \mimic\ and \ga\ show rapid initial improvement, while \sa\ exhibits more gradual improvement with fluctuations. This behavior can be explained by the algorithms' fundamental approaches: \mimic\ and \ga\ explore the solution space more broadly from the start, while \sa's exploration is more localized and dependent on its cooling schedule.
Fig. \ref{fig: four-peaks-iterations-vs-time-vs-size}  reveals that \mimic\ uses fewer iterations but takes significantly longer per iteration, especially for larger problem sizes. This is due to the computational intensity of its probabilistic model building and sampling processes. \ga, \rhc, and \sa\ use more iterations as problem size increases, but their execution times remain relatively low, reflecting their simpler update mechanisms.
These results highlight the trade-offs between solution quality and computational efficiency in the Four-Peaks problem. They demonstrate that population-based methods (\ga\ and \mimic) are more effective at finding high-quality solutions in this deceptive fitness landscape, but at the cost of increased computational complexity. The fourth hypothesis regarding \ga's performance with different population sizes and mutation rates will be addressed in the hyperparameter analysis section.

\section{Neural Network Weight Optimization \label{sec:neural-network}}  
This section explores the application of randomized optimization algorithms (\rhc, \sa, and \ga) to neural network weight optimization, comparing their performance against traditional backpropagation. The study uses the YouTube comment spam detection dataset from the previous classification project. The analysis focuses on evaluating these algorithms' performance in training neural networks, comparing their convergence behavior, accuracy, and computational efficiency, and examining the impact of various hyperparameters.
I \textbf{hypothesize} that:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Randomized optimization algorithms will not outperform backpropagation in terms of accuracy and computational efficiency.
    \item \sa\ and \ga\ will show better performance than \rhc\ in terms of accuracy, while \rhc\ will be faster than \sa\ and \ga. \sa\ is expected to provide a good balance between accuracy and execution time.
\end{itemize}
\subsection{Experimental Setup \label{sec:neural-network-experimental-setup}}
The neural network weight optimization was implemented using the \texttt{mlrose-ky} library, maintaining consistency with the architecture from Assignment 1 for fair comparison. Key components include:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Dataset}: YouTube comment spam detection dataset (reused from Assignment 1) and take the same testing dataset and training dataset.
    \item \textbf{Architecture}: 384 input nodes, one hidden layer with 100 nodes, and an output layer with 1 node for binary classification. \texttt{ReLU} activation for hidden layers and \texttt{sigmoid} for the output layer.
    \item \textbf{Algorithms and Hyperparameters}:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Fix Parameters: max iteration: 100; learning rate: 0.06; max attempts: 100; early stopping: True; random seed: 42
        \item \rhc Restarts: [5, 10, 20]
        \item \sa: Schedule: [\expD, \geomD, \arithD];  Max attempts: [10, 20, 50]
        \item \ga: Population size: [100, 200, 400]; Mutation probability: [0.1, 0.2, 0.3]
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}
The experimental procedure involved splitting the training dataset into subtraining (80\%) and validation (20\%) sets. Each algorithm was run with various hyperparameter combinations, with performance evaluated on both validation and test sets. The best model for each algorithm was selected based on validation performance (highest accuracy), with final comparisons made using test set performance.
It's important to note that in the  \texttt{mlrose-ky} implementation for neural network weight optimization, the loss function serves as the fitness function, with the goal of minimization. 

This setup allows for a comprehensive comparison of the randomized optimization algorithms against backpropagation, providing insights into their effectiveness for neural network training in the context of the spam detection task.

The results largely support our first hypothesis. \verb|BP| significantly outperforms the randomized optimization algorithms in both validation accuracy and execution time. This outcome aligns with expectations, given \verb|BP|'s use of gradient descent. \verb|BP|'s focused strategy of calculating how the loss function changes with respect to each weight provides a specific direction for adjustments, making it more effective than the exploratory approach of randomized methods, especially for networks with numerous parameters. Among the randomized optimization algorithms, \ga\ performs best with a validation accuracy of 0.812, followed by \rhc\ (0.572) and \sa\ (0.530). This partially contradicts our second hypothesis, which expected \sa\ to outperform \rhc. \ga's superior performance aligns with findings from the previous problems, where larger population sizes and smaller mutation probabilities yielded better results.
To better understand these outcomes, I analyzed the loss curves for each algorithm's best model (Fig. \ref{fig: nn-loss-curve}):
\begin{itemize}
    \item \rhc\ (Fig. \ref{fig: nn-loss-curve-rhc}) shows continuous loss reduction, suggesting potential for improvement with more iterations.
    \item \sa\ (Fig. \ref{fig: nn-loss-curve-sa}) doesn't show significant improvement before 400 iterations, possibly due to insufficient exploration of the weight space.
    \item \ga\ (Fig. \ref{fig: nn-loss-curve-ga}) demonstrates consistent loss decay. The loss reduces faster than \rhc\ and \sa, indicating \ga's ability to explore the weight space effectively.
\end{itemize}
\rhc's unexpected superiority over \sa\ may be due to the neural network weight space favoring \rhc's greedy search approach. With 10 restarts, \rhc\ can explore different parts of the weight space effectively. \sa's performance heavily depends on the cooling schedule, and the chosen maximum iteration of 1000 may be insufficient for finding the global optimum.
Analysis of the learning curve, comparing training and validation accuracy with iterations, reveals that \sa\ doesn't benefit from increased sample size. This may be because \sa\ is unable to adequately explore the weight space. This observation provides further insight into \sa's unexpected underperformance in this neural network optimization task. The fact that \sa\ doesn't show improved performance with more samples suggests that its exploration of the weight space is limited, possibly due to the cooling schedule or the way it generates neighbor solutions in the high-dimensional space of neural network weights.
These findings highlight the complexity of applying randomized optimization algorithms to neural network training. While they don't match \verb|BP|'s performance, they offer insights into the nature of the weight space and the challenges of non-gradient-based optimization in high-dimensional spaces.
The results also emphasize the importance of algorithm selection and hyperparameter tuning in neural network optimization. While \verb|BP| remains superior for this task, the relative performance of randomized methods can vary significantly based on their configuration and the specific characteristics of the problem.

\textbf{Impact of Hyperparameters}.
The analysis of hyperparameter impact on \rhc\ and \ga\ for neural network optimization reveals interesting patterns, consistent with our findings from previous problems.
\rhc's performance, as shown in Fig. \ref{fig: nn-rhc-best-fitness-restarts}, demonstrates a non-linear relationship with the number of restarts. The improvement from 5 to 10 restarts is substantial, but the gains diminish when moving from 10 to 20 restarts. This pattern suggests that while additional restarts help \rhc\ explore the weight space more thoroughly, there's a point of diminishing returns. This behavior is likely due to the vast and complex nature of the neural network's weight space, where even multiple restarts may not be sufficient to consistently find global optima.
\ga's performance (Fig. \ref{fig: nn-ga-best-fitness-schedule}) shows a more complex relationship with its hyperparameters. Interestingly, the largest population size doesn't always yield the best results, challenging the common assumption that larger populations are always better. This suggests that in the context of neural network optimization, there's an optimal balance between population diversity and computational efficiency. The generally negative impact of higher mutation rates across population sizes indicates that excessive mutation may be detrimental in this problem space, possibly disrupting good solutions more often than it helps escape local optima.
These findings for GA align with our earlier observations from the discrete optimization problems, reinforcing the idea that moderate population sizes and mutation rates often work best for complex optimization tasks.
Tab. \ref{table:nn-algorithm_performance} summarizes the performance comparison of the optimization algorithms for NN weight optimization. While \verb|BP| demonstrates superior performance across most metrics, it's noteworthy that among the randomized optimization algorithms, \ga\ stands out with remarkable results. \ga\ achieves the highest accuracy (0.814) and F1 score (0.827) among the randomized methods, approaching \verb|BP|'s performance. This success can be attributed to \ga's population-based approach, which allows it to explore a diverse set of solutions simultaneously. In the context of NN optimization, \ga's crossover operation can effectively combine promising weight configurations, while its mutation mechanism helps in escaping local optima - a common challenge in high-dimensional weight spaces. The relatively high recall (0.780) suggests GA's ability to find a broad range of correct weight configurations. In contrast, \rhc\ and \sa\ show significantly lower performance, which may struggle with the complexity of the neural network's weight space. 

These results emphasize the critical importance of algorithm selection and parameter tuning in neural network optimization. They also suggest that while gradient-based methods like backpropagation remain the gold standard, population-based approaches like GA may offer viable alternatives in certain scenarios.

\section{Summary}
This study explored randomized optimization algorithms across three domains: N-Queens, Four-Peaks, and neural network weight optimization for spam detection. The research revealed that algorithm performance varied significantly based on problem characteristics and size. In the N-Queens problem, \sa\ offered the best balance of solution quality and speed, while \ga\ found slightly better solutions at higher computational cost. For the Four-Peaks problem, population-based methods (\ga\ and \mimic) excelled in the deceptive landscape, with \ga\ providing a good balance between quality and efficiency. In neural network optimization, traditional backpropagation outperformed randomized methods, though \ga\ showed promise among the alternatives. Throughout the study, the importance of careful algorithm selection and hyperparameter tuning was evident. The research highlighted the complex relationships between algorithm performance, problem characteristics, and computational constraints, emphasizing that while some general trends hold, the effectiveness of randomized optimization methods is often more nuanced than initially hypothesized.
